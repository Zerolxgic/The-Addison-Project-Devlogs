It has been a little over a week since my last Addison devlog.

That was not because the project stopped moving.

It was almost the opposite.

The work got dense enough that I had to choose between keeping the build moving and keeping the public writing perfectly caught up. For a little while, I chose the work.

That left me with a different problem: how do I catch the devlog back up without turning it into a stiff changelog or a pile of internal phase names?

A lot has changed over the last week. Addison has moved further into the direction I have been aiming at for a while: less like a chatbot sitting beside the project, and more like a governed workspace agent that can eventually work inside a declared scope without losing the human authority layer underneath it.

That distinction matters to me.

I do not want Addison to become “an agent” in the vague hype sense. I want her to become useful in the practical sense. Something that can help carry the work, operate inside a bounded workspace, produce artifacts, keep track of state, and eventually act as a trusted deputy without pretending that trust means unlimited autonomy.

The hard part is that this kind of project has two jobs happening at the same time.

One job is building the system.

The other job is keeping the truth of the system honest.

It is easy to make progress sound bigger than it is. It is also easy to bury real progress under so much governance language that nobody can tell what actually changed. I am trying to land somewhere better than both of those.

So I am going to catch this devlog up in pieces.

This post is just the re-entry point.

The short version is: Addison kept moving. The architecture got more serious. The agent-workspace direction is becoming clearer. The governance model is starting to mature from maximum ceremony toward proportional trust.

But I still want to be careful about the claims.

A new capability seam is not the same thing as broad autonomy. A working layer is not the same thing as a finished system. A planned direction is not the same thing as accepted truth.

That has become one of the central lessons of this project.

Build fast enough to keep momentum.

Document carefully enough that the system stays honest.

And when the devlog falls behind, do not fake continuity. Rehydrate, catch up, and keep going.
