# Briefing 001 Before This Dev Log

I didn’t start this dev log at the beginning of Addison.

By the time I started writing here, a lot had already happened. A lot of building, a lot of correction, a lot of false starts, a lot of learning, and a lot of time spent trying to understand what this project actually needed to become.

So this first briefing is less about one clean starting line and more about the reality of how it began.

Addison did not come out of me sitting down with a polished master plan.

It came out of pressure.

Pressure from trying to carry too much in my own head.
Pressure from losing continuity between work sessions.
Pressure from feeling like I could see the shape of something deeper, but not yet having a structure strong enough to hold it.

What I was reaching for early on was not just “an AI project.”

I was trying to build a system that could hold direction with me over time. Something that could help carry thought, work, memory, and continuity forward in a way that felt more solid than starting over every time context got interrupted.

In the beginning, a lot of that was still instinct.

I knew what I was reaching toward more than I knew how to explain it. I could feel the pull of the project before I had the right language for it. So the early phases were a lot of exploration, testing, reframing, and trying to separate what was actually essential from what only looked exciting on the surface.

That process mattered.

Because one of the biggest things I had to learn early was that I was not just building a tool. I was building around a way of thinking. Around continuity. Around memory. Around structure. Around the need for the work to keep its shape over time.

That took a while to understand.

A lot of the early project life was spent trying to figure out what Addison really was, what it wasn’t, and what kind of discipline it would need if it was going to become something real instead of just another idea that felt powerful in the moment.

So even though this dev log starts later, the project itself has a much longer runway behind it.

This repo is not the full record of every step.

It’s more like the point where I stopped keeping so much of the process internal and started trying to write from inside the build while it was still alive.

That matters to me, because a big part of this whole project has been learning that things become more real when they stop living only in my head.

That is true for Addison.

And honestly, it is true for me too.
