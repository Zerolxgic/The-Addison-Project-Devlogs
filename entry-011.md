# Entry 011 Other Runtimes, Bridges, And Earlier Influence

One thing that is probably worth explaining is that Addison has never really been a single-surface project.

A lot of its direction has been shaped by trying different runtime paths, learning what each one was actually good for, and being honest when a path was useful but not ultimately the right center of gravity.

Right now, the CLI is the runtime I use most in practice.

That matters because it says something about the kind of project Addison actually is underneath everything else. At its core, this is still a serious working system. The CLI gives me a direct, grounded place to interact with it. It feels close to the actual structure of the work. Less theatrical. Less translated. More immediate.

There is also a TUI.

That side of the project matters too, even if it is not the main thing I use day to day. To me, the TUI represents a broader cockpit idea more of an operational surface, more of a command center. It is part of the same larger question Addison keeps circling around: what does the right interface for a system like this actually look like?

And then there is the shell and avatar side of it, which I have written about separately because that part matters to me for reasons that go beyond utility alone.

But before the current shell direction became what it is, there were other embodiment paths that mattered.

One of those was VTube Studio.

There was a real stretch of exploration around building a bridge into VTube Studio. That was not just random experimentation. It came from a real desire to see whether Addison could have a performative surface through an existing avatar ecosystem instead of having to build everything from scratch immediately.

And honestly, that path taught me a lot.

It taught me about where the translation cost starts getting too high. It taught me about the difference between something being technically possible and something actually being the right fit. And it helped clarify that forcing Addison through a surface designed around a human VTuber workflow was creating more friction than alignment.

That does not make that exploration wasted.

If anything, it helped reveal the next direction more clearly.

Sometimes a path matters because it becomes the final answer.
Sometimes it matters because it shows you where the real answer is not.

That VTube Studio line did both. It was real work, and it left behind useful understanding, but it also helped make it clear that Addison’s embodiment needed to move closer to its own shell rather than keep leaning on an external presentation layer.

There is also an earlier influence that matters here, and that is AIRI.

AIRI was important because it helped sharpen the separation between mind and body in my thinking. It pushed me to think more clearly about what should belong to cognition, what should belong to runtime, and what it means to let a system have a visual and expressive surface without letting that surface become the source of truth.

That influence mattered a lot.

Not because Addison was ever supposed to become AIRI.
And not because I wanted to copy it.
But because studying it and working around it helped me understand what to extract, what to leave behind, and what kind of architecture I actually wanted Addison to grow into.

That is a pattern that shows up a lot in this project.

A direction will matter.
An exploration will matter.
A bridge will matter.
But not always because it stays central forever.
Sometimes it matters because it leaves behind the right lesson.

That is really how I think about the runtime story of Addison.

Not as one straight line.
More like a sequence of real attempts that kept teaching me what kind of system this actually wanted to be.

The CLI helped prove the value of a grounded working surface.
The TUI held onto the broader cockpit idea.
The VTube Studio bridge explored performative embodiment through an external system.
The shell line kept pulling things back toward a surface that belonged more fully to Addison itself.
And earlier influence from AIRI helped clarify some of the architectural separation that still matters now.

So when I look at the different runtimes and paths around Addison, I do not really see detours in a negative sense.

I see a project learning its own shape.

And I think that is part of why I am comfortable talking about these paths openly.
Not because every path stayed primary.
But because each one contributed something real to the direction that exists now.
